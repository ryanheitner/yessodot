The crisis

When the best available care isn't enough

In the wake of October 7 and ongoing military operations, Israel faces a quiet mental health emergency. A generation of elite, highly trained veterans is sidelined by what clinicians call the Polytrauma Triad: post-traumatic stress disorder, mild traumatic brain injury, and chronic pain.

These conditions are biologically linked — forming a cycle of disability that conventional SSRIs and talk therapy often cannot break. These veterans have received the best care available, and still cannot return to functional life.

The science now points toward a new frontier. Yesodot exists to bring it home.

The science

What the research shows

Ibogaine is not a fringe treatment. It is the subject of serious clinical attention at the world's leading institutions — and Israel is positioned to lead what comes next.

Stanford University

Documented "life-changing" reductions in PTSD, depression, and anxiety among U.S. Special Operations veterans treated with ibogaine — often after a single treatment.

University of Texas at Austin

Completed a 40-person observational ibogaine study and launched an $18 million dedicated psychedelic research center to advance the science further.

U.S. federal investment

In 2025, Texas allocated $50 million for ibogaine research, matched by $50 million from a drug development partner — a $100 million public-private commitment to FDA-approved drug development.

How it works

Ibogaine is a naturally occurring alkaloid from the iboga plant, native to Central Africa. Researchers hypothesize its effects on neuroplasticity may allow the brain to "reset" patterns of trauma, addiction, and chronic pain — particularly when paired with clinical preparation and integration.
